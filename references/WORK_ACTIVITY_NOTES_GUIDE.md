# Work Activity Notes Synthesis Guide
## From Raw Transcript to Work Activity Notes

This is a comprehensive guide for synthesizing work activity notes from raw contextual inquiry data (interview transcripts and observation notes). This process is critical for successful contextual analysis and building a Work Activity Affinity Diagram (WAAD).

## Overview

**Purpose**: Transform verbose, unstructured raw interview transcripts into concise, self-contained work activity notes that capture the essence of user work practice, concerns, and needs.

**Key Principle**: Work activity notes should be so well synthesized that the team never has to go back to raw data to answer questions, fill in blanks, or determine context.

**Perspective**: Maintain a **work domain perspective** (focus on observed work practice), NOT requirements or design. Stay with what users do and experience, not what the system should be.

---

## Key Definitions

### Work Activity Note
A work activity note documents **a single point about a single concept, topic, or issue** synthesized from raw contextual data. Notes are stated as simple, succinct declarative points in the user's perspective.

### Work Role
A collection of responsibilities defined by a job title or work assignment. Examples: ticket seller, ticket buyer, event manager, database administrator.

### Work Activity Affinity Diagram (WAAD)
A hierarchical diagram used to sort and organize work activity notes, pulling together notes with similarities and common themes to highlight common work patterns across all users.

### Data Bin
A temporary repository (labeled pile of notes) to hold data organized by category or topic.

## The Process

### Step 1: Transcription
- Convert audio/video recordings to written transcripts
- Maintain confidentiality agreements when using external services
- Keep transcripts as raw as recorded

### Step 2: Interpretation Sessions
**Team Setup:**
- Gather interpretation group: interviewers, note takers, core UX team
- One or more sessions to review all interview data
- User researchers recount interviews to the rest of the team

**Session Activities:**
- Review interview/observation notes and recordings
- Retell events and observations
- Discuss and capture key points, issues, design ideas
- Note missing data and questions
- Create/refine flow model sketches
- Synthesize work activity notes

**Group Structure:**
- Start with one big session for alignment
- Break into parallel groups for efficiency
- Each group has a moderator
- User researchers give accounts of each interview
- Design-informing modelers work on flow model
- Note takers create work activity notes
- Reconvene to consolidate findings

### Step 3: Work Activity Note Synthesis
This is where raw data transforms into structured notes following specific guidelines (see next section).

### Step 4: Organization into Data Bins
Sort notes into labeled categories as you create them:
- User and user class information
- Social aspects of work practice
- Emotional impact and phenomenological aspects
- Task-specific information
- Physical work environment
- Design inspiration ideas
- Open questions
- Issues for discussion

## Critical Guidelines

### 1. **Tag Each Note**
```
Format: [SourceID]-[NoteNumber] [WorkRole]
Example: U10-42 [Faculty Advisor]
```
- **Source ID**: Unique identifier of the person interviewed/observed
- **Work Role**: The role associated with this note
- Essential for tracing back to source if questions arise

### 2. **Paraphrase and Synthesize (Don't Quote Verbatim)**
❌ **Bad** (Verbatim quote):
> "Ah, they just, they sign and mark, let me see if I have one that I can pull up, it's like that, they've changed it. But here they mark like satisfactory or unsatisfactory. It's like applied from the date that they sign. And mark satisfactory, unsatisfactory and then the date. And students can have one unsatisfactory and still pass the exam."

✅ **Good** (Synthesized):
> "At the conclusion of a research defense exam, each faculty member signs and dates an exam card to indicate if the student's performance was satisfactory or unsatisfactory."

> "A student passes a research defense exam if he or she earns 'satisfactory' from all or all-but-one of the research committee."

**Key Points:**
- Maintain user's perspective and intentions
- Don't introduce new content
- Keep expression terse and to the point
- Abstract clear and concise statements

### 3. **Make Declarative Statements (Not Q&A Format)**
Questions from the interviewer confirmed by the user should be reworded as if they came from the user.

❌ **Bad**:
> "Q: Do you prefer to sit with friends? A: Yes, when I buy tickets I like to sit together."

✅ **Good**:
> "When buying student tickets to MU basketball, I look for an option allowing several friends to sit together."

### 4. **Filter Noise and Fluff**
Raw data is too verbose. Boil it down to essence.

❌ **Bad** (Full of noise):
> "I think it should, I think it should go to the faculty advisor electronically because, you know, the campus mail could take a couple of days to reach."

✅ **Good** (Filtered):
> "Exam notecards are currently sent to faculty advisors via campus mail, but could take a couple of days to reach them."

> "Exam notecard should be sent to the faculty advisor electronically [design idea]."

### 5. **Be Brief: 1-3 Succinct Sentences**
Embrace breviloquence; eschew grandiloquence.

Maximum length: Three sentences
Ideal length: One to two sentences

### 6. **One Concept Per Note**
Each note should contain just one concept, idea, or fact, with possibly one rationale statement.

❌ **Bad** (Multiple concepts):
> "I don't ask for printed confirmations because I'm afraid someone might use my credit card, and also the printer is often broken, plus I prefer email anyway."

✅ **Good** (Split into separate notes):
> "I do not ask for printed confirmations of ticket transactions because I am afraid someone else might find it and use my credit card number."

> "The ticket kiosk printer is frequently out of order."

> "I prefer to receive ticket confirmations via email rather than printed receipts."

### 7. **Make Each Note Complete and Self-Standing**
Notes will be shuffled, sorted, and separated. Each must be independently understandable.

❌ **Bad** (Depends on context):
> "They use it for that purpose."

✅ **Good** (Self-contained):
> "Event managers use the MUTTS database to track ticket sales by venue."

### 8. **Never Use Indefinite Pronouns Without Clear Referents**
Never use "this," "it," "they," or "them" unless the referent is identified in the same note.

❌ **Bad**:
> "This is difficult during peak hours."

✅ **Good**:
> "Getting adequate help from the ticket seller is difficult during peak hours."

### 9. **State Work Role Instead of Pronouns**
❌ **Bad**:
> "She checks the database first."

✅ **Good**:
> "The ticket seller checks the database first."

Or if in user's perspective:
> "As a ticket seller, I check the database first."

### 10. **Add Disambiguating Words**
When breaking raw data into multiple notes, add context that would be lost.

**Raw data**:
> "When I am looking to buy student tickets to MU basketball, I like to look at different seating options vs. prices; I sometimes look for an option allowing several friends to sit together."

✅ **Good** (Context preserved in both notes):
> "When I am looking to buy student tickets to MU basketball, I like to look at different seating options vs. prices."

> "When I am looking to buy student tickets to MU basketball, I sometimes look for an option allowing several friends to sit together."

Note: The italicized context "When I am looking to buy student tickets to MU basketball" was added to the second note to maintain clarity when separated.

### 11. **Avoid Repetition**
Don't duplicate information that goes elsewhere:
- Work role names → Go in flow model
- User characteristics → Go in user class definitions
- Work activity specifics → Go in work activity notes

## Detailed Examples

### Example 1: MUTTS Ticket Buying System

**Raw Data (User Comment)**:
> "It is too difficult to get enough information about events from a ticket seller at the ticket window. For example, sometimes I want to see information about popular events that are showing downtown this week. I always get the feeling that there are other good events that I can choose from but I just do not know which ones are available and the ticket seller usually is not willing or able to help much, especially when the ticket window is busy. Also, it is hard to judge just from the information available at the ticket window whether it has been well received by others."

**Synthesized Work Activity Notes** [Ticket Buyer]:

1. "It is too difficult to get enough information about events from a ticket seller at the ticket window."

2. "I want to know about current and popular events."

3. "I would like to be able to find my own events and not depend on the ticket seller to do all the browsing and searching."

4. "There are potential communication gaps because the ticket seller does not always understand my needs."

5. "During peak times, the level of personal attention from the ticket seller is minimal."

6. "It would be nice to get reviews and other feedback from people who have already seen the show."

7. "[Design idea] Consider including capability for people to add reviews and to rate reviews. Question: Should this capability be located at the event venues rather than the kiosk?"

### Example 2: Group Ticket Purchase Scenario

**Raw Data (User Comment)**:
> "Last Friday, several friends and I were planning a birthday outing for our friend, Suzy. We decided to get tickets to Juxtaposition, the MU a cappella group's show, and I volunteered to pick up tickets on my way home. When I got to the ticket office there was a long line and when it was my turn I found out we could not get eight seats next to each other. I did not know if I should get tickets that are not next to each other or try for a different event. I needed to call my friends on the phone but knew I would be holding up everyone else in the line. I finally got out of the line to make the call and it took a lot more time."

**Synthesized Work Activity Notes** [Ticket Buyer]:

1. "Sometimes I need to coordinate events and tickets with friends who are not with me."

2. "Sometimes I need to buy a set of tickets with adjacent seating."

3. "[Design idea] Consider an option in the kiosk to 'Find best n adjacent seats.'"

4. "Taking time to coordinate ticket buying for groups potentially slows down everyone in the line."

5. "[Design question] Can we add anything to the kiosk that would facilitate group collaboration and communication? How about, at least, sending confirmation of ticket purchase to group members?"

### Example 3: Short Raw Data Transformation

**Raw Data**:
> "I think it should go to the faculty advisor electronically because, you know, the campus mail could take a couple of days to reach."

**Context from Further Questioning**: This is about exam notecards sent before the exam.

**Synthesized Work Activity Notes** [Graduate Student]:

1. "Exam notecard goes to faculty advisor before exam."

2. "Exam notecards are currently sent to faculty advisors via campus mail, but could take a couple of days to reach them."

3. "Exam notecard should be sent to the faculty advisor electronically [design idea]."

### Example 4: User Perspective vs. Factual Perspective

**Raw Data**:
> "I think of sports events as social events, so I like to go with my friends. The problem is that we often have to sit in different places, so it is not as much fun. It would be better if we could sit together."

**User's Perspective**:
> "When I am looking to buy student tickets to MU basketball, I look for an option allowing several friends to sit together."

**Factual Perspective**:
> "Many students who buy MU basketball tickets want the option to sit with their friends."

**Note**: Either perspective is acceptable, but be consistent within your project. User's perspective is generally preferred for affinity diagramming.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### ❌ Mistake 1: Designer/Implementer Bias
**Problem**: Rejecting notes because "it won't be implemented in this version" or "that's technically impossible."

**Solution**: Stay focused on user work practice and needs. Don't prematurely filter based on technical constraints. There may be alternative solutions.

**Example**:
- User need: "I want to know when the next bus arrives"
- Wrong thinking: "We can't track buses in real-time, reject this note"
- Right thinking: "This is a valid need. We could display average bus arrival frequencies by route."

### ❌ Mistake 2: Premature Technology Decisions
**Problem**: Locking into specific technology solutions too early (e.g., "all forms must be electronic").

**Solution**: Stay flexible. Different scenarios may need different solutions.

**Example**:
- Electronic forms work well for distributed signature collection
- Paper forms might still be best when all signers are in the same room (e.g., thesis defense)

### ❌ Mistake 3: Verbatim Copying
**Problem**: Copy-pasting from raw data without synthesis.

**Why it fails**: Results in verbose, noisy notes that require multiple readings to understand.

### ❌ Mistake 4: Multiple Concepts in One Note
**Problem**: Cramming multiple ideas into one note.

**Why it fails**: During WAAD building, the note can only go in one place, losing other valuable concepts.

### ❌ Mistake 5: Context-Dependent Notes
**Problem**: Using pronouns or references that only make sense with surrounding text.

**Why it fails**: Notes get separated during sorting and become meaningless.

### ❌ Mistake 6: Vague Labels
**Problem**: Using low-descriptive-power wording like "miscellaneous," "general," "other things."

**Solution**: Be specific and precise. Bad: "How we validate information" → Good: "How we validate forms"

### ❌ Mistake 7: Jumping to Requirements or Design
**Problem**: Writing notes like "System should..." or "We need a button that..."

**Solution**: Stay in work domain perspective. Focus on what users do and experience, not what the system should do.

## Data Categories

Organize your work activity notes into these typical categories (extend as needed):

### 1. User and User Class Information
- Demographics, skills, knowledge, experience
- Physical characteristics or limitations
- Work role responsibilities

### 2. Social Aspects of Work Practice
- How people interact with each other
- Collaboration patterns
- Communication flows
- Social influence on work

### 3. Emotional Impact and Phenomenological Aspects
- User feelings and emotions
- Long-term user experience factors
- Satisfaction and frustration points

### 4. Task-Specific Information
- Specific activities users perform
- Task sequences and workflows
- Task frequency and importance

### 5. Physical Work Environment
- Workspace characteristics
- Lighting, noise, interruptions
- Physical constraints and requirements

### 6. Design Inspiration Ideas
- Innovative ideas from users
- Design suggestions
- "Wouldn't it be nice if..." statements
- **Label these clearly**: [Design idea] or [Design question]

### 7. Open Questions
- Questions arising during analysis
- Things to ask in next interviews
- Ambiguities needing clarification

### 8. Data Holes
- Missing data discovered
- Information needed to complete the picture
- Drives further data collection

## Quality Checklist

Before finalizing each work activity note, verify:

- [ ] **Tagged**: Has source ID and work role
- [ ] **Synthesized**: Not verbatim quote; essence captured
- [ ] **Declarative**: Statement format, not Q&A
- [ ] **Filtered**: No noise or unnecessary verbiage
- [ ] **Brief**: 1-3 sentences maximum
- [ ] **Single concept**: One idea or fact (+ optional rationale)
- [ ] **Self-standing**: Understandable without other notes
- [ ] **No dangling pronouns**: All referents clear within the note
- [ ] **Work role stated**: Not "he/she"
- [ ] **Disambiguated**: Context added where needed
- [ ] **Work domain focus**: Not requirements or design (unless labeled as such)
- [ ] **No repetition**: Info not duplicated from flow model or user classes
- [ ] **Specific and precise**: Avoids vague wording

## Special Note Types

### Design Ideas
When users suggest design features or you observe design opportunities:

**Format**: [Design idea] followed by the suggestion

**Example**:
> "[Design idea] Consider an option in the kiosk to 'Find best n adjacent seats.'"

### Design Questions
When analysis raises design questions:

**Format**: [Design question] followed by the question

**Example**:
> "[Design question] Can we add anything to the kiosk that would facilitate group collaboration? How about sending confirmation to group members?"

### Data Holes
When missing information is discovered:

**Format**: [Data hole] followed by what's missing

**Example**:
> "[Data hole] Need to understand how ticket sellers currently handle group reservations during peak hours."

## Printing and Physical Format

### Printing Options
1. **Post-it Notes**: Print on yellow Post-it label stock (6 per page)
2. **Bond Paper**: Print on colored paper, 6 notes per page, cut into pieces
3. **Handwritten**: Acceptable, use consistent format

### Color Coding
- **Yellow or White**: Work activity notes
- **Other colors**: Reserve for WAAD labels and special note types
- **Different colors/angles**: Design ideas, questions, data holes

## Process Tips

### Speed and Efficiency
- Initial sessions: Go slowly and sequentially for team alignment
- Later sessions: Work in parallel once everyone understands the process
- Deal notes in small batches (e.g., 20 per person) initially
- Don't over-refine; diminishing returns come quickly

### Team Collaboration
- No note ownership; anyone can place or move notes
- Share rationale for note placement decisions
- Allow interruptions for clarifications
- Brainstorm together for ambiguous cases

### Iteration and Refinement
- Edit notes on the spot if needed
- Split notes when multiple concepts discovered
- Add handwritten notes during WAAD building if necessary
- Don't make up data; note holes for future collection

---

## Success Criteria

Your work activity notes are successful if:

1. **Self-sufficient**: Team never needs to return to raw data for clarity
2. **Scannable**: Key point understood at a glance
3. **Portable**: Note makes sense when moved to any location
4. **Atomic**: Contains one clear concept
5. **Actionable**: Provides basis for requirements and design
6. **User-centered**: Maintains user perspective and work domain focus

---

## Next Steps After Note Synthesis

Once work activity notes are created:

1. **Print notes** (if not handwritten)
2. **Organize into data bins** by category
3. **Build Work Activity Affinity Diagram (WAAD)**
   - Group notes by affinity
   - Create hierarchical labels
   - Identify patterns and themes
4. **Conduct WAAD walkthrough** with stakeholders
5. **Extract requirements** (Chapter 5)
6. **Build design-informing models** (Chapter 6)

## Quick Reference: Good vs. Bad Examples

| Raw Data | ❌ Bad Note | ✅ Good Note |
|----------|------------|-------------|
| "They check it first" | "They check it first." | "The ticket seller checks the database for seat availability before quoting prices to the ticket buyer." |
| "It's confusing during busy times" | "It's confusing during busy times." | "Coordinating group ticket purchases at the ticket window is confusing during peak hours." |
| Long rambling quote | [Full verbatim quote] | [Synthesized essence in 1-2 sentences] |
| Multiple ideas in user's comment | Single note with all ideas | Separate note for each distinct idea |
| "The system should email confirmations" | "The system should email confirmations." | "I would like to receive ticket confirmations via email." [work domain, not design] |

## Remember

> "Work activity notes should represent raw data so well that the team never has to go back to the raw data to answer questions, fill in blanks, determine what the real point was, or to sort out context."

The quality of your work activity notes determines the success of your WAAD and ultimately the quality of your requirements and design. Invest time in getting them right.
